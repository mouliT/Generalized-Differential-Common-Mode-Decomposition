% ============================================================
\section{How Asymmetry Couples the Two Modes}
% ============================================================

Section~1 defined the DM/CM quantities and built the transformation
matrices.  Now we put those matrices to work on the actual circuit
equations.  The central question is:

\begin{center}
\emph{If you apply $\TiN$ and $\TvN$ to the $N$-line circuit
equations, what structure emerges — and what changes when one
capacitor is $kC$ instead of $C$?}
\end{center}

The answer splits into two cases.  For a \emph{symmetric} system
($k = 1$, all capacitors equal) the transformed equations break
into $N-1$ independent DM equations and one CM equation — the two
modes are electrically invisible to each other.  For an
\emph{asymmetric} system ($k \neq 1$) the capacitor mismatch
injects a coupling term: a DM voltage oscillation drives a CM
current directly.  This DM-to-CM conversion is the root cause of
asymmetry-induced conducted EMI.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The N-Line Circuit Equations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The goal of this section is to write down the circuit equations in
the original per-line variables before any DM--CM transformation.
We build them up one component at a time — no matrices yet.

% ---- single line ----
\subsubsection*{Step 1 — Pick One Line and Label It}

Look at \figref{fig:P1Fig1} and single out \emph{line~$n$}.
It contains three circuit elements in the path from the left input
terminal to ground:

\begin{circuitbox}[Elements on Line $n$ (from left to right)]
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Series resistor $R$:} models wiring resistance,
          bond-wire resistance, busbar loss.
    \item \textbf{Series inductor $L$:} models trace or cable
          inductance.
    \item \textbf{Shunt capacitor $C_n$:} parasitic capacitance
          from line~$n$ to the grounded baseplate.
          Lines $1$ to $N-1$ have $C_n = C$;
          line~$N$ has $C_N = kC$ (the asymmetric one).
\end{itemize}
Current $i_n$ flows left to right through $R$ and $L$, then
down through $C_n$ to ground.
\end{circuitbox}

% ---- left terminal voltage ----
\subsubsection*{Step 2 — What Is the Driving Voltage?}

The left terminal of line~$n$ sits at voltage $v_{nP}$ with
respect to the floating reference~$P$.
But $P$ is not ground — it is at $v_{Pg}$ above ground
(recall \S1.2).  So the voltage of line~$n$'s left terminal
\emph{with respect to ground} is:
\[
    v_{n,\mathrm{gnd}}
    = \underbrace{v_{nP}}_{\text{line to }P}
    + \underbrace{v_{Pg}}_{P\text{ to ground}}
    = v_{nP} + v_{Pg}.
\]
This is the total voltage that drives current through the series
$R$--$L$--$C_n$ path to ground.

% ---- EMI approximation ----
\begin{physbox}[EMI-Frequency Approximation: Why $i_n$ Flows Entirely Through $C_n$]
Look carefully at Fig.~\ref{fig:P1Fig1}.  In general, line $n$ carries
\emph{two} currents: $i_n$ entering from the left, and $i_n'$ leaving
to the right toward the load.  By KCL at the junction above $C_n$:
\[
    i_{C_n} = i_n - i_n'.
\]
The \emph{exact} capacitor voltage drop is therefore
$v_{C_n} = (i_n - i_n')/(p\,C_n)$, \emph{not} simply $i_n/(p\,C_n)$.

\textbf{Why can we use $i_n$ alone?}  At EMI frequencies (tens of kHz
and above), the shunt capacitor $C_n$ looks like a near-short circuit:
\[
    \frac{1}{\omega C_n} \ll Z_{\mathrm{load}}.
\]
The high-frequency current strongly prefers the low-impedance
cap-to-ground path over the load path.  As a result,
\[
    i_n' \approx 0
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    i_{C_n} \approx i_n.
\]
The paper carries $i_n'$ through the full derivation for generality.
We do the same: the equations below are written in their exact form.
Where the EMI approximation $i_n' \approx 0$ simplifies the result,
it is noted explicitly.
\end{physbox}

% ---- three voltage drops ----
\subsubsection*{Step 3 — Write the Voltage Drop Across Each Element}

\textbf{Resistor} $R$: by Ohm's law the voltage drop is
\[
    v_R = R\, i_n.
\]

\textbf{Inductor} $L$: by Faraday's law the voltage drop is
\[
    v_L = L\,\frac{d\,i_n}{d\,t}.
\]
Shorthand: define the operator $p \triangleq d/dt$
(called the \emph{Heaviside operator}).  Then
$v_L = pL\cdot i_n$.  Every time you see $p$ in front of
something, read it as ``multiply by $d/dt$''.

\textbf{Capacitor} $C_n$: by KCL (see physbox above), only
$(i_n - i_n')$ flows through $C_n$.  The capacitor accumulates
charge $Q = \int (i_n - i_n')\,dt$, so its terminal voltage is
\[
    v_{C_n} = \frac{i_n - i_n'}{p\,C_n}.
\]
At EMI frequencies $i_n' \approx 0$, so $v_{C_n} \approx i_n/(pC_n)$,
but we retain the general form.

% ---- KVL ----
\subsubsection*{Step 4 — Apply KVL}

The driving voltage equals the sum of the three drops:
\[
    v_{nP} + v_{Pg}
    = \underbrace{R\,i_n}_{v_R}
    + \underbrace{pL\cdot i_n}_{v_L}
    + \underbrace{\dfrac{i_n - i_n'}{p\,C_n}}_{v_{C_n}}.
\]

\begin{eqnbox}[Per-Line KVL — One Equation for Line $n$ (Eq.~9 in paper)]
\begin{equation}
    v_{nP} + v_{Pg}
    = (R + pL)\,i_n + \frac{i_n - i_n'}{p\,C_n}
    \label{eq:KVL_one}
\end{equation}
\end{eqnbox}

This is the fundamental equation.  Everything that follows is just
writing it $N$ times and packing it into a matrix.

% ---- elastance ----
\subsubsection*{Step 5 — Introduce Elastance $S_n = 1/C_n$}

Define the \emph{elastance} of the shunt cap on line~$n$:
\[
    S_n \triangleq \frac{1}{C_n}.
\]
Then $\dfrac{1}{pC_n} = \dfrac{S_n}{p}$, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:KVL_one} becomes:
\[
    v_{nP} + v_{Pg}
    = (R + pL)\,i_n + \frac{S_n}{p}\,(i_n - i_n').
\]

\begin{physbox}[Why Bother Naming $1/C_n$ as $S_n$?]
In the per-line equation, $1/C_n$ always appears together with
the operator $1/p$.  When we stack $N$ equations into a matrix
(Step~6 below), the $1/p$ factor will appear \emph{outside} the
matrix, while the capacitances appear \emph{inside} as matrix entries.
Writing $S_n$ keeps the two roles cleanly separated:
\[
    \frac{S_n}{p}\cdot i_n
    \;\longrightarrow\;
    \frac{1}{p}\,\mathbf{S}_N\,\mathbf{i}.
\]
The matrix $\mathbf{S}_N$ holds the circuit parameters;
$1/p$ is the integrating operator that sits in front.
\end{physbox}

The numerical values of the elastances in the $N$-line model are:
\[
    S_1 = S_2 = \cdots = S_{N-1} = \frac{1}{C},
    \qquad
    S_N = \frac{1}{kC}.
\]

% ---- N equations ----
\subsubsection*{Step 6 — Write the Equation for Every Line}

Applying Eq.~\eqref{eq:KVL_one} to each of the $N$ lines:
\begin{align*}
    v_{1P} + v_{Pg}
        &= \Bigl(R + pL + \tfrac{1}{pC}\Bigr)\,i_1 \\[4pt]
    v_{2P} + v_{Pg}
        &= \Bigl(R + pL + \tfrac{1}{pC}\Bigr)\,i_2 \\[4pt]
        &\;\vdots \\[4pt]
    v_{(N-1)P} + v_{Pg}
        &= \Bigl(R + pL + \tfrac{1}{pC}\Bigr)\,i_{N-1} \\[4pt]
    v_{NP} + v_{Pg}
        &= \Bigl(R + pL + \tfrac{1}{kpC}\Bigr)\,i_N
\end{align*}

Three things to notice:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $v_{Pg}$ appears in \textbf{every} equation — it is a
          common additive term on the left of all $N$ lines.
    \item The coefficient $(R + pL)$ is \textbf{identical} across
          all $N$ lines.
    \item The cap term differs \textbf{only on the last line}:
          $1/(pC)$ for lines 1 to $N-1$, but $1/(kpC)$ for line $N$.
\end{itemize}

% ---- matrix stacking ----
\subsubsection*{Step 7 — Stack into a Matrix Equation}

Collect the $N$ unknowns and $N$ driving voltages into column
vectors:
\[
    \mathbf{i} = \begin{bmatrix} i_1 \\ \vdots \\ i_N \end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{v}_{nP} = \begin{bmatrix} v_{1P} \\ \vdots \\ v_{NP} \end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{1}_N = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{i}' = \begin{bmatrix} i_1' \\ \vdots \\ i_N' \end{bmatrix}
    \quad\text{(load current vector)}.
\]

The shared term $v_{Pg}$ multiplies the all-ones vector
$\mathbf{1}_N$, because it appears on the left of \emph{every}
equation.  The right-hand side has a coefficient matrix
$\mathbf{Z}$ that encodes both the $R$--$L$ and the cap
contributions.  Since $(R + pL)$ is the same on every line, it
contributes $(R + pL)\,\mathbf{I}_N$ (scalar times identity).
The cap terms differ line by line, so they contribute
$(1/p)\,\mathbf{S}_N$ where $\mathbf{S}_N$ is a diagonal matrix
of elastances.  Putting it all together:

\begin{eqnbox}[Mixed-Mode Circuit Equations — Eq.~9 in paper]
\begin{equation}
    (R + pL)\,\mathbf{i}
    + \frac{1}{p}\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{i}')
    = \mathbf{v}_{nP} + v_{Pg}\,\mathbf{1}_N
    \label{eq:MMeqn}
\end{equation}
where
\[
    \mathbf{S}_N
    = \operatorname{diag}\!\Bigl(
        \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{C},\;\ldots,\;\tfrac{1}{C}}_{N-1},\;
        \tfrac{1}{kC}
      \Bigr).
\]
\end{eqnbox}

This is just $N$ copies of Eq.~\eqref{eq:KVL_one} written
at once.  No new physics — only compact notation.

\textit{Note — why ``mixed-mode''?}  Each per-line current $i_n$
is a blend of both DM and CM contributions (recall the inverse
transform in \S1.6).  This equation is written in those
\emph{un-decomposed} per-line variables, so DM and CM are mixed
together inside every $i_n$ and $v_{nP}$.  The next subsection
applies the transformation to separate them.

\begin{statebox}[Two Observations About the Circuit Equation]
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$(R+pL)\mathbf{I}_N$ and the cap term $(1/p)\mathbf{S}_N$ are separate.}
          The $R$--$L$ term multiplies $\mathbf{i}$ alone; the cap term acts on
          $(\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{i}')$.  Equivalently, defining
          $\mathbf{Z} = (R+pL)\mathbf{I}_N + (1/p)\mathbf{S}_N$, the equation reads
          $\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{v}_{nP} + v_{Pg}\mathbf{1}_N
          + (1/p)\mathbf{S}_N\,\mathbf{i}'$ — same $\mathbf{Z}$, with an extra
          $\mathbf{i}'$ source term.
    \item \textbf{$\mathbf{S}_N$ is diagonal but not symmetric}
          when $k \neq 1$.  All entries equal $1/C$ except the
          last, which is $1/(kC)$.  This is the \textbf{only}
          source of asymmetry in the entire equation — and
          therefore the only possible source of DM--CM coupling
          after the transformation.
\end{itemize}
\end{statebox}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Applying the DM--CM Transformation}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{physbox}[Why Transform? — The Motivation]
The mixed-mode equation~\eqref{eq:MMeqn} is correct, but it is written
in \emph{per-line} variables $(i_1, \ldots, i_N)$ and
$(v_{1P}, \ldots, v_{NP})$.  Nobody in EMC measures per-line currents
directly — a conducted-emissions test measures CM current, and a
switching converter generates DM voltage.  The question we actually
want to answer is: \emph{``a DM voltage drives the circuit; how much
CM current results?''}  That answer is buried and unreadable in the
per-line form.

The fix is a \textbf{change of coordinates}: replace the per-line
variables with the DM and CM variables defined in \S1.  The
transformation matrices $\TiN$ and $\TvN$ are not new objects — they
are exactly the DM/CM definitions from \S1 written as matrices.
Applying them to both sides of Eq.~\eqref{eq:MMeqn} is the
\emph{same physics}, re-expressed in a language where DM and CM are
explicit.  The payoff: if the circuit is symmetric the resulting
impedance matrix $\mathbf{Z}_\mathrm{DCM}$ is diagonal (no coupling);
if it is asymmetric, off-diagonal entries appear — and those
\emph{are} the DM--CM coupling, now directly readable.
\end{physbox}

Multiply Eq.~\eqref{eq:MMeqn} on the left by $\TvN$, substitute
$\mathbf{i} = (\TiN)^{-1}\iDCM$ and
$\mathbf{i}' = (\TiN)^{-1}\mathbf{i}'_{\mathrm{DCM}}$:
\[
    \underbrace{\TvN\,\mathbf{Z}\,(\TiN)^{-1}}_{\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}}}
    \;\iDCM
    \;=\;
    \vDCM
    \;+\; v_{Pg}\,\mathbf{e}_N
    \;+\; \frac{1}{p}\,\underbrace{\TvN\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\TiN)^{-1}}
                                   _{\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}}
    \,\mathbf{i}'_{\mathrm{DCM}}.
\]

\begin{eqnbox}[DCM Equations — Eq.~10 in paper]
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}}\,\iDCM
    = \vDCM + v_{Pg}\,\mathbf{e}_N
    + \frac{1}{p}\,\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}\,\mathbf{i}'_{\mathrm{DCM}}
    \label{eq:DCMeqn}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}} = \TvN\,\mathbf{Z}\,(\TiN)^{-1}$,\;
$\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}} = \TvN\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\TiN)^{-1}$,\;
and $\mathbf{e}_N = [0,\ldots,0,1]^T$.\\[4pt]
\textit{EMI approximation:} when $i_n' \approx 0$, the last term
vanishes and the equation reduces to
$\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}}\,\iDCM = \vDCM + v_{Pg}\,\mathbf{e}_N$.
\end{eqnbox}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{Expanding $\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}}$ — Eqs.~11--13 in paper}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The impedance matrix $\mathbf{Z}$ has two parts: the resistive/inductive
part $(R + pL)\,\mathbf{I}_N$ (the same on every line) and the capacitive
part $(1/p)\,\mathbf{S}_N$ (which carries the $k \neq 1$ asymmetry).
Substituting $\mathbf{Z} = (R + pL)\,\mathbf{I}_N + (1/p)\,\mathbf{S}_N$
into $\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}} = \TvN\,\mathbf{Z}\,(\TiN)^{-1}$ and
using linearity of matrix multiplication:

\begin{eqnbox}[Expanded DCM Impedance — Eq.~11 in paper]
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{DCM}}
    = (R + pL)\,\underbrace{\TvN\,(\TiN)^{-1}}_{\displaystyle\mathbf{M}}
    \;+\; \frac{1}{p}\,\underbrace{\TvN\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\TiN)^{-1}}
                                   {\displaystyle\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}}
    \label{eq:Zdcm_expanded}
\end{equation}
Two new matrices appear: $\mathbf{M}$ (carries $R$ and $L$) and
$\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}$ (carries the capacitors, including the asymmetry
$k$).  Their definitions are Eqs.~12--13 of the paper.
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{eqnbox}[$\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}$ — Eqs.~12--13 in paper]
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{M}
        &= \TvN\,(\TiN)^{-1}
        \label{eq:Mdef} \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}
        &= \TvN\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\TiN)^{-1}
        \label{eq:Sdcm_def}
\end{align}
\end{eqnbox}

\paragraph{$\mathbf{M}$ is always diagonal — $R$ and $L$ never couple DM to CM.}

For $N = 2$, compute directly:
\[
    \mathbf{M}
    = \begin{bmatrix}1 & -1 \\[3pt] \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2}\end{bmatrix}
      \begin{bmatrix}1 & \tfrac{1}{2} \\[3pt] -1 & \tfrac{1}{2}\end{bmatrix}
    = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 0 \\[3pt] 0 & \tfrac{1}{2}\end{bmatrix}.
\]
The result is diagonal with entries $\Ni = N = 2$ (DM row) and $1/N = 1/2$
(CM row).  This holds for all $N$:
\[
    \mathbf{M}
    = \operatorname{diag}\!\bigl(\underbrace{\Ni,\,\ldots,\,\Ni}_{N-1},\;
      \tfrac{1}{N}\bigr).
\]

\begin{circuitbox}[Physical Meaning of $\mathbf{M}$]
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DM row:} $R_{\mathrm{DM}} = \Ni R = NR$,\;
          $L_{\mathrm{DM}} = NL$ — DM current circulates through two
          lines in series, so impedance doubles (for $N = 2$). \checkmark
    \item \textbf{CM row:} $R_{\mathrm{CM}} = R/N$,\;
          $L_{\mathrm{CM}} = L/N$ — CM current splits equally across
          all $N$ lines in parallel, so impedance divides. \checkmark
    \item \textbf{No off-diagonal entries, for any $k$.}
          The $R$--$L$ branch is identical on every line, so it can
          never distinguish DM from CM — it can never cause coupling.
\end{itemize}
\end{circuitbox}

\paragraph{$\mathbf{S}_{\mathrm{DCM}}$ depends on $k$ — this is where coupling lives.}

Because $\mathbf{S}_N = \operatorname{diag}(1/C,\ldots,1/C,\,1/(kC))$
is \emph{not} a scalar multiple of the identity when $k \neq 1$, the
product $\TvN\,\mathbf{S}_N\,(\TiN)^{-1}$ will generally have
off-diagonal entries.  Those off-diagonal entries are the
\textbf{DM--CM coupling elastances} — directly readable once we are in
the DCM basis.
