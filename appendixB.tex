% ============================================================
\section{Change of Basis: Geometric Interpretation}
% ============================================================

This section provides a geometric interpretation of similarity
transformation $A_{\text{new}} = P^{-1}AP$, then contrasts it with the
circuit transformation $Z_{\text{new}} = T^v Z (T^i)^{-1}$.

% ---- B.1 ----
\subsection{The Same Vector, Different Coordinates}
\label{sec:same-vector}

A fundamental insight from linear algebra: a vector is a geometric
object — an arrow in space — that exists independently of any coordinate
system. When we change basis, we are not moving the vector; we are only
changing how we describe it numerically.

\begin{center}
\textit{The arrow stays fixed. Only the numbers change.}
\end{center}

Consider $\mathbb{R}^2$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Standard basis:
    $
        e_1 =
        \begin{bmatrix}
        1\\0
        \end{bmatrix},
        \;
        e_2 =
        \begin{bmatrix}
        0\\1
        \end{bmatrix}
    $

    \item New basis:
    $
        e'_1 =
        \begin{bmatrix}
        1\\1
        \end{bmatrix},
        \;
        e'_2 =
        \begin{bmatrix}
        -1\\1
        \end{bmatrix}
    $
\end{itemize}

A vector $\mathbf{x}$ pointing in some direction can be written as

\[
\mathbf{x} = 3e_1 + 2e_2
\]

or equivalently

\[
\mathbf{x} = 2.5e'_1 - 0.5e'_2
\]

The arrow is identical. The coordinates $(3,2)$ and $(2.5,-0.5)$
describe the same geometric object in different coordinate systems.

% ---- B.2 ----
\subsection{Change of Basis Matrix}
\label{sec:change-of-basis}

Let $v = \{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n\}$ be the old basis and
$v' = \{e'_1, e'_2, \ldots, e'_n\}$ be the new basis.

The change-of-basis matrix
$
M_{v' \to v}
$
converts coordinates expressed in the new basis to coordinates in the
old basis:

\[
\mathbf{x}_v = M_{v' \to v}\mathbf{x}_{v'}
\]

For convenience, define

\[
M \equiv M_{v' \to v}.
\]

\textbf{How to build $M$:}

Each column $j$ of $M$ contains the coordinates of the new basis vector
$e'_j$ written in the old basis.

\textbf{Example:}

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ with

\[
e'_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\1
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
e'_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
-1\\1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

(expressed in the standard basis)

\[
M =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 \\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

To convert coordinates in the opposite direction,

\[
\mathbf{x}_{v'} = M^{-1}\mathbf{x}_v.
\]

% ---- B.3 ----
\subsection{Linear Transformation as a Physical Action}
\label{sec:linear-transformation}

Now consider a linear transformation $T$ — a physical operation such as
rotation, scaling, or projection. The transformation itself does not
depend on any basis; it represents the same physical action regardless
of how we choose to describe vectors.

When we represent $T$ as a matrix, we are simply expressing the same
operation in a particular coordinate system.

\textbf{How to compute the matrix $[T]_v$:}

Apply $T$ to each basis vector and express the result in the same basis.

\begin{itemize}
\item Column 1 of $[T]_v$ = coordinates of $T(e_1)$ in basis $v$
\item Column 2 of $[T]_v$ = coordinates of $T(e_2)$ in basis $v$
\item etc.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Example:}

Let $T$ be a $90^\circ$ counter-clockwise rotation.

\[
T(e_1) =
T
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\0
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0\\1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
T(e_2) =
T
\begin{bmatrix}
0\\1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
-1\\0
\end{bmatrix}
\]

Thus

\[
[T]_v =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -1 \\
1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

The matrix representation changes when the basis changes, but the
underlying transformation remains the same.

% ---- B.4 ----
\subsection{Deriving $B = M^{-1}AM$ Geometrically}
\label{sec:derive-similarity}

Suppose a transformation $T$ is represented by matrix $A$ in basis $v$.
We want the matrix $B$ that represents the same transformation in the
new basis $v'$.

\begin{enumerate}

\item Start with coordinates in the new basis:

\[
\mathbf{x}_{v'}
\]

\item Convert to the old basis:

\[
\mathbf{x}_v = M\mathbf{x}_{v'}
\]

\item Apply the transformation:

\[
\mathbf{y}_v = A\mathbf{x}_v = AM\mathbf{x}_{v'}
\]

\item Convert the result back to the new basis:

\[
\mathbf{y}_{v'} = M^{-1}\mathbf{y}_v
                 = M^{-1}AM\mathbf{x}_{v'}
\]

\end{enumerate}

But by definition,

\[
\mathbf{y}_{v'} = B\mathbf{x}_{v'}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\boxed{B = M^{-1}AM}
\]

This is the similarity transformation. The same matrix $M$ appears on
both sides because the domain and codomain are the same vector space.

% ---- B.5 ----
\subsection{Same Space vs. Different Physical Quantities}
\label{sec:same-vs-different}

The derivation above assumes the transformation maps a space to itself:

\[
\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}
\]

Both $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$ belong to the same coordinate space.

\bigskip

In contrast, the impedance matrix relates two different physical
quantities:

\[
\mathbf{v} = Z\mathbf{i}
\]

Currents and voltages are both vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, but they
represent different physical variables governed by different laws:

\begin{itemize}
\item Currents obey KCL (current conservation)
\item Voltages obey KVL (loop voltage relations)
\end{itemize}

When changing coordinates, each quantity must be transformed using its
own transformation matrix:

\begin{itemize}
\item $T^i$ transforms current coordinates
\item $T^v$ transforms voltage coordinates
\end{itemize}

The impedance matrix therefore transforms as

\[
Z_{\text{new}} = T^v Z (T^i)^{-1}
\]

\textbf{Why this is not a similarity transformation}

\begin{itemize}
\item Similarity: $A_{\text{new}} = P^{-1}AP$
\item Circuit: $Z_{\text{new}} = T^v Z (T^i)^{-1}$
\end{itemize}

Two different matrices appear because the input and output variables
represent different physical quantities.

% ---- B.6 ----
\subsection{Numerical Example: Similarity Transformation}
\label{sec:numerical-similarity}

\[
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 1\\
0 & 2
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
P =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1\\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
P^{-1} =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1\\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
A_{\text{new}}
=
P^{-1}AP
=
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 2\\
0 & 2
\end{bmatrix}
\]

The same matrix $P$ appears on both sides because the transformation
acts within a single vector space.

% ---- B.7 ----
\subsection{Numerical Example: Circuit Transformation}
\label{sec:numerical-circuit}

\[
Z =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 0\\
0 & 6
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
T^i =
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac12 & -\frac12\\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
(T^i)^{-1} =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & \frac12\\
-1 & \frac12
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
T^v =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1\\
\frac12 & \frac12
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
Z_{\text{DCM}}
=
T^v Z (T^i)^{-1}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
8 & -2\\
-2 & 2
\end{bmatrix}
\]

The off-diagonal entries indicate coupling between differential and
common modes due to circuit asymmetry.

For a symmetric case ($Z_1 = Z_2$)

\[
Z_{\text{DCM}} =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 0\\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

which is diagonal (no DM–CM coupling).

% ---- B.8 ----
\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{|p{3cm}|p{5.5cm}|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Aspect} & \textbf{Similarity Transform} & \textbf{Circuit Transform} \\
\hline
\textbf{Map type} & $A : V \to V$ & $Z : i \to v$ \\
\hline
\textbf{Spaces} & Same coordinate space & Different physical variables \\
\hline
\textbf{Matrices} & Same $P$ on both sides & $T^i,\;T^v$ \\
\hline
\textbf{Formula} & $A_{\text{new}} = P^{-1}AP$ & $Z_{\text{new}} = T^v Z (T^i)^{-1}$ \\
\hline
\textbf{Physical meaning} & Same transformation expressed in new coordinates & Change of current/voltage coordinate representation \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The geometric interpretation clarifies the difference: similarity
transformation simply re-expresses a transformation in another basis,
while the circuit transformation changes the coordinate descriptions of
two different physical variables.
